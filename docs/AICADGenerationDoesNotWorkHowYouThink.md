# AI CAD generation in production: mostly not what you think

**The skepticism is largely warranted.** As of early 2026, no major CAD vendor ships neural-network-based 3D model generation, and the only true text-to-CAD system with meaningful production deployment — Zoo.dev — works reliably only for simple mechanical parts. What the industry markets as "AI-powered CAD" falls into three categories that are often deliberately conflated: topology optimization rebranded as "generative design" (shipping for years, not neural-net-based), workflow assistants and copilots (essentially documentation chatbots), and actual LLM-based CAD generation (one shipping product, a handful of early startups, zero verified enterprise manufacturing deployments). The gap between demo and production remains enormous, and the architectural reasons for this gap are instructive.

## The only shipping text-to-CAD system and how it actually works

Zoo.dev (formerly KittyCAD) is the single most substantive production text-to-CAD system available today. Founded in 2021 by Jessie Frazelle (ex-GitHub, Microsoft, Google) and Jordan Noone (co-founder of Relativity Space), Zoo launched its Text-to-CAD alpha in **December 2023** and shipped Zoo Design Studio v1.0 in **May 2025**. The company reports **300,000 early users**, over **1 million unique Text-to-CAD designs generated**, and **2 million+ hours** of geometry engine computation. Pricing ranges from a free tier to $99/month (Pro) and $399/month per seat (Team), with enterprise custom pricing available.

The architecture is the most revealing part. Zoo **does not generate B-rep geometry directly from a neural network**. Instead, it uses an LLM-based agentic pipeline: the user submits a natural language prompt, the system generates KCL code (Zoo's proprietary domain-specific CAD language), that code executes against Zoo's proprietary GPU-native geometry engine, and the engine produces B-rep output exportable as STEP, glTF, STL, and other formats. The January 2026 release of "Zookeeper" extends this into a full conversational agent that plans, writes KCL, executes it, inspects the resulting geometry via multi-view snapshots, debugs errors, and iterates — architecturally similar to Codex or Claude Code but pointed at a CAD kernel instead of a compiler.

This code-generation-through-kernel-execution pattern is **the universal architecture for every production and research CAD generation system**. No system anywhere generates B-rep directly from neural network weights into a shipping product. The reason is fundamental: code preserves parametric editability and design intent, while direct neural B-rep generation (explored in research systems like DeepCAD, SolidGen, and BRepGen) produces frozen, non-editable geometry with no feature tree — useless for real engineering workflows.

Zoo's candor about limitations is notable. Their own documentation states that **"traditional, simple mechanical parts work best right now: fasteners, bearings, connectors."** The system is explicitly stochastic — the same prompt produces different results across runs, and failing prompts sometimes succeed on retry. CEO Frazelle deliberately avoids calling it "AI," preferring "ML," and writes: "Our version of AI is the day you can type 'Make me a rocket' and get a nozzle off the build plate ready for hot-fire first try. Until then, it's ML." Independent testing by Xometry Pro confirmed that Zoo delivers accurate, editable models for simple functional parts but **"results dropped off sharply with medium and high-complexity designs."**

Enterprise fine-tuning is confirmed as a production capability — Zoo states it fine-tunes models for "several enterprise customers" using their NX, Creo, CATIA, or SolidWorks files converted to KCL. However, the only published case study (RMFG, a sheet metal startup) uses Zoo's **file conversion API**, not Text-to-CAD. No named enterprise customer is publicly confirmed as using text-to-CAD in production manufacturing.

## What major CAD vendors are actually shipping versus claiming

The contrast between marketing language and shipping reality across the major CAD vendors is stark. Every incumbent has announced "AI-powered" features, but **none ship neural-network-based CAD generation**. What they do ship falls into three buckets.

**Workflow automation features** represent the most genuinely useful AI shipping today. Autodesk Fusion 360's AutoConstrain (January 2025) uses ML trained on sketch data to predict and apply geometric constraints — a real time-saver that eliminates tedious manual constraining. Siemens NX's Command Prediction and Selection Prediction use ML to anticipate the user's next action. Dassault's SOLIDWORKS 2025 ships a Command Predictor, fastener recognition, auto-generated drawings, and mate assistance. These are **incremental productivity tools**, not revolutionary generation capabilities, but they're genuinely useful and genuinely shipping.

**Documentation chatbots branded as "copilots"** are the second category. Siemens Design Copilot NX (powered by **Microsoft Phi-3**) answers questions about NX workflows. Onshape AI Advisor (powered by **Amazon Bedrock**) provides step-by-step Onshape guidance — and explicitly states it "does not generate designs, predict errors in real time, or make complex engineering decisions." Dassault's AURA assistant is in beta, trained on SOLIDWORKS documentation. These are help-desk chatbots embedded in the application, not design tools.

**Topology optimization marketed as "generative design"** is the third and most misleading category. Autodesk's Fusion 360 "Generative Design" (shipping since 2018), PTC's Creo Generative Topology Optimization and GDX, and Siemens NX's Topology Optimizer all use **FEA-based density-gradient methods** — classical physics-based optimization that iteratively removes material to meet structural constraints. PTC's GDX adds evolutionary/genetic search across multiple topology optimization runs. These tools require engineers to fully specify loads, constraints, materials, and manufacturing methods upfront. They are powerful and mature but are **not neural-network-based and not what anyone means by "AI-generated CAD."** A 2019 research survey stated this explicitly: "Current generative design does not use state-of-the-art AI technology (e.g., deep learning)."

The most forward-looking incumbent effort is **Autodesk's Project Bernini** — a 3-billion-parameter wavelet latent diffusion model trained on 10 million 3D shapes. It generates 3D meshes from text prompts. It is strictly a research project with no commercial integration, trained on data that explicitly prohibits commercial use. Autodesk previewed "Neural CAD" features in Fusion at AU 2025, demonstrating text-to-native-editable-geometry, but with clear disclaimers that it is "not a product specification, and release timing is still subject to change."

## The startup landscape is smaller and earlier than expected

The user asked about several companies — Hayden AI, MakerVerse, Rosetta AI, CADmium, and Atlas3D — as potential CAD generation players. Research reveals that **most of these have nothing to do with CAD generation**. Hayden AI is a traffic enforcement company using computer vision on buses. Rosetta AI is a Taiwanese e-commerce personalization SaaS. MakerVerse is a Siemens/ZEISS joint venture for manufacturing sourcing — it analyzes uploaded CAD files for quoting, but generates no geometry. CADmium is an open-source browser-based parametric CAD tool (interesting but has zero AI components). Atlas3D is either a Vienna gaming-asset startup or a NeurIPS academic paper, neither doing engineering CAD.

The actual startup landscape for true text-to-parametric-CAD production systems is thin:

- **Zoo.dev** ($10M raised, ~30 employees): The category leader, described above. LLM → KCL → proprietary kernel → B-rep.
- **Backflip** ($30M Series A from a16z/NEA, December 2024): Founded by Markforged founders. Generates 3D-printable meshes from text/image/sketch prompts, with an emerging scan-to-parametric-CAD tool that converts point clouds to SOLIDWORKS-native files. Claims training on **100 million+ synthetic 3D geometries**. The mesh generator works; the parametric CAD tool is in alpha.
- **Adam AI** ($4.1M seed, Y Combinator, January 2025): Text-to-parametric-CAD editor, available as web app and Onshape plugin. Four employees. Very early.
- **Katalyst Labs** (recently unveiled): Open-source text-to-CAD using CadQuery + LLM APIs + RAG. Generates parametric designs that update when parameters change.

Adjacent but architecturally different are **computational/algorithmic design** companies. nTopology ($105M+ raised) ships mature implicit-modeling and topology-optimization software used by Ford, Lockheed Martin, and Honeywell — but this is deterministic algorithmic design, not neural generation. LEAP 71 has algorithmically designed and **hot-fired real rocket engines**, but their Noyron system is explicitly rule-based and physics-driven, not neural-network-based. Hyperganic ships voxel-based algorithmic design for additive manufacturing. These are impressive engineering tools, but they require engineers to code the design algorithms — they are not "type a prompt, get a part."

## How the production inference pipeline actually works

Every shipping and near-shipping CAD generation system follows the same fundamental architecture: **stochastic code generation → deterministic kernel execution → validation loop**. This three-phase pattern is not incidental — it reflects a deep technical constraint.

In the **stochastic phase**, an LLM generates code in a domain-specific language (Zoo's KCL, CadQuery wrapping OpenCascade, OpenSCAD, or FreeCAD Python macros). Temperature sampling means the same prompt yields different code on each run. In the **deterministic phase**, a CAD kernel (Zoo's proprietary engine, OpenCascade, or CGAL) executes that code and produces exact B-rep geometry — the same code always yields identical geometry. In the **validation phase**, the system checks whether the geometry is valid (watertight, manifold, reasonable dimensions). If execution fails, error messages feed back to the LLM for self-correction.

Zoo's Zookeeper extends this into a full agentic loop: plan → generate KCL → execute → take multi-view snapshots → visually inspect geometry → debug → iterate. Research systems add visual language models (VLMs) for verification: CADCodeVerify generates questions about the rendered geometry, answers them, and provides corrective feedback to the LLM, achieving measurable accuracy improvements.

**No production system uses retrieval-augmented generation for geometry generation itself**, though Zoo's agent retrieves KCL documentation during operation. No production system automatically validates design-for-manufacturability — Zoo states its models are "tuned to generate mechanical components for real world manufacturing processes" through training data bias, not explicit constraint checking. No production system guarantees manufacturability or structural performance.

The integration pattern with CAD kernels is always indirect: **AI → code/parameters → kernel API → geometry**. No neural network writes directly to Parasolid, ACIS, or OpenCascade data structures. This indirection is what makes the approach viable — it leverages decades of proven kernel engineering while letting the neural network handle only the creative/generative step of writing code.

## Generative design and generative CAD are architecturally unrelated

This distinction matters enormously and is systematically obscured by vendor marketing. **Generative design** (topology optimization) starts with a fully specified engineering problem — loads, constraints, materials, manufacturing methods, design envelope — and uses gradient-based mathematical optimization to find the material distribution that minimizes an objective function like weight or compliance. It is deterministic (same inputs → same output), physics-grounded, requires extensive human setup, and has shipped in production for nearly a decade. It outputs one or more optimized geometries within the engineer's specified constraints.

**Generative AI CAD** starts with a natural language description and uses a neural network to generate geometry that matches that description. It is stochastic (same input → different outputs), trained on data rather than physics, requires minimal setup, and has shipped in one production system since late 2023. It outputs a starting-point geometry that requires human refinement.

The practical implications diverge completely. Topology optimization results from Autodesk Fusion or PTC Creo carry engineering meaning — they satisfy specified load cases with known safety factors. AI-generated CAD from Zoo carries no engineering guarantees — the geometry might look correct but has not been validated against any physics. Topology optimization requires the engineer to already know the problem well enough to specify it quantitatively. AI generation requires only a verbal description but produces something that must be fully validated afterward. These are complementary tools for different phases of the design process, not competing approaches to the same problem.

nTopology occupies a distinct architectural niche — implicit modeling with field-driven design. Geometry is represented as continuous signed distance functions (equations, not meshes or B-rep) that can be directly driven by simulation fields. An engineer can say "stiffen this plate where vibration amplitude is highest" and the system deterministically generates geometry from the stress field. This is more expressive than topology optimization but still rule-based and deterministic, not neural.

## Enterprise adoption is real for optimization, fictional for generation

The evidence splits cleanly. **Topology optimization has genuine, documented enterprise adoption**: ISUZU achieved **43% weight reduction** in diesel engine gears using Autodesk Fusion's generative design. NASA/Jacobs designed a Mars Sample Return lid. Cummins uses PTC Creo's simulation-driven generative design. Airbus partnered with Neural Concept to reduce aerodynamic prediction time from one hour to **30 milliseconds**. These are verified, published case studies with named companies and quantified results.

**Text-to-CAD has zero verified enterprise manufacturing deployments.** Zoo's enterprise fine-tuning claim ("several enterprise customers") is unverified externally. The only published Zoo case study (RMFG) uses file conversion, not generation. No automotive, aerospace, or manufacturing company has publicly confirmed using text-to-CAD output in production parts. The total funding raised by all text-to-CAD startups combined is roughly **$45 million** — less than a single Series B for many AI companies.

Independent testing and user feedback consistently describe text-to-CAD as useful for **quick prototyping, concept exploration, and education** but not for production engineering. Engineers report that AI handles simple, well-specified parametric parts (a 20-tooth helical gear with exact dimensions) but fails on complex assemblies, vague requirements, or precision-dependent components. The typical workflow is: generate a starting point → extensively refine in traditional CAD → run separate simulation/validation → create production drawings manually. Human intervention remains at **60–80%+** of the total design effort.

The broader AI adoption context reinforces the skepticism. McKinsey reports only **6% of companies** qualify as AI high performers. ISG found only **31% of enterprise AI use cases** reached full production in 2025. And 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% in 2024. The "design" category accounts for just **7% of departmental AI spend** ($511 million of $7.3 billion), and most of that is visualization and ideation tools, not CAD generation.

## Conclusion

The honest state of AI CAD generation in production is this: **one real product works for simple parts, topology optimization works well and has for years, and everything else is either a documentation chatbot or a research prototype**. Zoo.dev has built something genuinely novel — a from-scratch geometry kernel, a purpose-built CAD language, and an agentic LLM pipeline that produces real, editable, parametric B-rep geometry. But it works reliably only for fasteners, brackets, connectors, and similarly simple components. The major CAD vendors are shipping useful but mundane AI features (auto-constraining, command prediction, auto-drawings) while their text-to-CAD efforts remain in research. The most commercially impactful "AI" in CAD — topology optimization — predates the current neural-network wave by a decade and uses no neural networks at all.

The architectural lesson is that the code-generation-through-kernel-execution pattern is the only viable production approach. Direct neural B-rep generation remains a research problem. The code intermediary (KCL, CadQuery, OpenSCAD) preserves parametric editability, leverages proven kernel engineering, and gives the LLM a tractable code-generation task rather than an intractable geometry-synthesis task. But this same indirection means the system inherits all the reliability limitations of LLM code generation — stochastic outputs, prompt sensitivity, failure on complex specifications, and no engineering guarantees.

For anyone evaluating these tools: topology optimization for structural optimization is mature and proven. AI workflow assistants from major vendors provide genuine but incremental productivity gains. Text-to-CAD from Zoo or Backflip is a useful prototyping aid for simple parts. None of these replace an engineer with a traditional CAD tool for production work. The "Cursor for CAD" copilot model — AI augmenting an engineer's existing workflow rather than generating from scratch — appears to be the most viable near-term path, and it is where the smartest startups and incumbents alike are converging.
